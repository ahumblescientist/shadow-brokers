About sharks

before thinking of a solution we need to explain the problem briefly and what obligated us to build this solution and why even is this problem important.

Across the world’s oceans, more than 500 species of sharks have lived for millions of years, playing a vital role in keeping marine ecosystems balanced. Sharks regulate the populations of prey species, and without them the entire food web could be disrupted. They also keep prey populations healthy, as many sharks target weak, sick, or injured individuals, which prevents the spread of disease and ensures that only the strongest fish reproduce. In addition, sharks protect important habitats: for example, tiger sharks patrol seagrass beds and prevent herbivores like turtles and dugongs from overgrazing, helping these habitats remain healthy and supporting countless other marine species.

Solutions for predecting sharks

Since we are dealing with large amounts of data, we decided to train a (ML) machine learning model to deal with this data and detecated our model for a specific month for predecting the probabilty of sharks appearing in a certain region, we can train this model using more data as a future improvment suggession.

Due to challenges with insufficient and uncorrelated direct shark data, we utilized available NASA satellite data—specifically Sea-Surface Temperature (SST), Chlorophyll-a, Sea-Surface Salinity (SSS), and Sea-Surface Height (SSH)—to train a machine learning model that establishes a connection between these environmental variables and the probability of shark presence.

We integrated this data into a web page using Leaflet.js to create an interactive heat map, which visualizes areas with varying probabilities of shark presence. The same heat map was also used to display the distribution of each individual feature. Additionally, we leveraged the outputs of our machine learning model to provide probability predictions based on user-inputted feature values.

Solutions for protecting sharks

Sharks face numerous threats to their survival. Here, we will discuss potential solutions for a selection of these identified dangers.

The primary threat to sharks is overfishing and shark finning, where sharks’ fins are removed for consumption, often leaving the still-living body to be discarded back into the ocean. To address this threat, governments should implement measures such as restricting fishing during sharks’ reproduction seasons (which vary by species), establishing protected habitats, and enforcing strict regulations on finning, as it is both wasteful and inhumane.

The second significant threat is starvation due to the decline of prey species, often caused by overfishing of smaller fish. To mitigate this, we propose using data from our tracking devices to monitor areas where sharks have not had sufficient food over a given period. In such cases, food could be supplied either from fish shop waste or by introducing fertilized fish eggs into suitable habitats to promote prey growth. This approach relies on aggregated data from multiple sharks, as depending on a single individual could yield inaccurate results and lead to food waste.
