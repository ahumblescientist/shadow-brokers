Final probability — What You’re Seeing

We developed a probability-based framework to estimate shark presence by combining four environmental features derived from NASA satellite data. Instead of focusing on eddy dynamics, the model uses data analysis and machine learning to link oceanographic conditions with the likelihood of shark activity.

Selected Features:

Surface Currents (speed/arrows): Show the direction and strength of flow that transports material and reveals eddy boundaries.

Sea Surface Temperature (SST): defines preferred thermal habitats.

Sea Surface Height (SSH): linked to currents and productivity zones.

Strain Magnitude (|S|): Kinematic front strength. Higher |S| highlights sharper, more active edges without using thermal or biological variables.

Photosynthetically Available Radiation (PAR): energy available for primary production.

How to read hotspots: Prioritize areas where W ≳ 0 (eddy edges) overlaps with high |S| and elevated EKE. These zones are dynamically active and frequently align with increased shark encounter probability.

SST Features — What You’re Seeing

Why SST matters: Sea-surface temperature (SST) shapes habitat preference and creates thermal fronts—sharp temperature changes that organize flow and can aggregate prey. Sharks often patrol along these fronts rather than uniform water masses.

What you’re seeing (heatmap layers):

SST (colors/contours): Warmer/cooler water masses and isotherm patterns.

SST Gradient Magnitude ∣ ∣∇SST∣: A pure thermal front strength metric—larger values = sharper, more active thermal edges.

(Optional) Laplacian SST^2: Highlights ribbons/cores of fronts (sign changes mark frontal bands and their curvature).

How to read hotspots: ocus on thermal edges where ∣∇SST∣ is high and isotherms crowd together. These dynamically active fronts are strong candidates for higher shark encounter probability.

Chlorophyll Features — What You’re Seeing

Why Chlorophyll-a matters:

Chlorophyll-a is a direct proxy for primary productivity (phytoplankton biomass). High Chl or sharp bio-fronts (strong spatial contrasts) often signal abundant small prey (zooplankton/forage fish), which can attract top predators like sharks.

What you’re seeing (heatmap layers):

Chl-a (colors/contours): Productive vs. less-productive water.

Bio-front strength

∣∇Chl∣ or ∣∇ln(Chl)∣: Purely biological “edge strength”—larger values = sharper, more active prey contrasts. (Optional) Laplacian

How to read hotspots: Prioritize high-Chl bands that co-occur with strong, coherent gradients

SSS Features — What You’re Seeing

Why SSS matters: Sea-surface salinity (SSS) traces freshwater–ocean mixing (rain, rivers, ice melt) and submesoscale/mesoscale stirring. Sharp haline fronts (strong salinity contrasts) organize surface flow and can concentrate prey—useful cues for shark hotspot scouting.

SSH Features — What You’re Seeing

Why SSH matters: Sea-surface height (SSH) reveals eddy structure: cores (peaks/troughs in the sea surface) and edges/fronts where flow intensifies and mixing concentrates prey. Eddy rims often align with higher shark encounter probability.

What you’re seeing (heatmap layers):

SSH (colors/contours): Peaks/troughs mark eddy cores; tightly packed contours highlight edges/fronts.

Geostrophic Currents (speed/arrows): Currents inferred from SSH gradients that trace the eddy rim and transport material.

SSH Gradient Magnitude ∣∇η∣: A height-only “front strength” metric—larger values = sharper, more active eddy edges.

How to read hotspots: Focus on thermal edges where ∣∇SST∣ is high and isotherms crowd together. These dynamically active fronts are strong candidates for higher shark encounter probability.
